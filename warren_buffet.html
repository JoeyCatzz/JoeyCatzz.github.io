Warren Buffet

Stock picking strategy

The business must possess a moat

For Warren to find a business appealing, it must be protected by a moat either in terms of its intangible assets such as a strong brand, network efficiency, cost advantages, high replacement costs or high barriers of entry.

The business must be predictable

As companies that possess a moat are well-protected, these businesses are more stable than those that do not have a competitive advantage and thus constantly fight against other ‘unprotected’ businesses.
Warren loves simple, stable and predictable companies, evident in a long history of growing revenues, earnings, and high returns on capital.

The business must have pricing power

It’s alfa-omega for a business’ long-term success that it can increase its prices at a rate that at least matches inflation. It often requires strong brand loyalty, an indispensable product, a well-protected industry or the like. If a company offers a product/service that consumers continue to consume despite annual price increases, that business possesses pricing power.

The business must be swimming in cash

A business with the ability to generate a lot of cash from its operations/assets. Warren prefers companies with low CAPEX requirements and a conservative capital structure.

The business must have a great and shareholder-friendly management team

The management team must run the business as if they owned the entire enterprise.
Capital allocation is the ‘science’ on how to best add shareholder value from the company’s profits.
Basically, there are only two options:
1) retain earnings in the company with the purpose of re-investing it back into the business, or
2) return capital to shareholders through dividend or share buybacks.

The business must be offerd at an attractive price

Even the most amazing business can be a bad investment if you pay too high a price for it.
Keep in mind that “the price you pay determines your rate of return.
Therefore, always be sure that you have a wide margin of safety between the purchase price and intrinsic value.

Financial Statements

What Warren Buffet is looking for in an income statement

  1. Net earnings show great consistency (upward trend) over the last 10 years.
  2. Gross Profit margin > 40%. Companies with a Gross Profit margin of 40% or better tend to be companies with some sort of durable competitive advantage.
  3. Net margin > 20%
  4. Interest Payment / Operating Profit * 100 < 15%
  5. Per Share Earnings show an upward trend over the last 10 years

What Warren Buffet is looking for in a balancesheet

  1. Retained earnings show great consistency over the last 10 years.
  2. Return on equity (ROE)
    3. > 25%
  3. Little to no long term debt: long term debt / 4 is smaller then net earnings
    The company can pay back it's long term debt within 4 years.
  4. Debt to Shareholders' Equity Ratio

What Warren Buffet is looking for in a cashflow statement

  1. capital expanditures/net earnings less than 50% or 25%
  2. high augmentend payout ratio, stock buybacks are prefered

When to Sell?

  1. You need more money for an even better investment.
  2. When a company may lose it's competitive advantage.
  3. During Crazy Bullmarkets. P/E ratio of 40 or higher.
