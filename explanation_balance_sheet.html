Assets & Current Asset Cycle

This is where all the goodies are kept: the cash, the plant and equipment, the patents, ...
The balance sheet are divided in two groups: Current Assets, and All Other Assets. Current Assets can be or will be converted into cash in a very short period of time (usually within a year). As a rule, they are listed on the balance sheet in order of their liquidity.
Current Assets are also known as quick, liquid, or floating assets.

Current Assets Cycle

Current assets are also referred to as the "working assets" of the business because they are in the cycle of cash going buy inventory; Inventory is then sold to vendors and becomes Accounts Receivable. Accounts Receivable, when collected from the vendors, they turn back into Cash. This cycle repeats itself over and over again, and it is how a business makes money.

three-month Treasuries (U.S. governent security with a constant maturity period of 3 months), or other highly liquid assets.

Total Inventory

Inventory is the company's products that it has warehoused to sell to its vendors.

Net Receivables

Money owed to the company.
When a company sells its products to a purchaser, it does so on the basis of either cash up front or payment due thirty days after the purchaser receives the goods. In some businesses the cash isn't due for even longer periods. Sales in this state are called receivables.

Prepaid Expenses

Businesses sometimes pay for goods and services that they will receive in the near future. They are payed for so they are assets of the business.

Other Current Assets

These are non-cash assets that are due within the year but are not yet in the company's hands.
For instance: deferred income tax recoveries.

Total Current Assets and the Current Ratio

Current Ratio = current assets/current liabilities
It is believed that if the outcome is below 1 this is a bad thing. How ever strong companies have such strong earning powers that they can easily cover there current liabilities. Also, they have no problem tapping into the cheap, short term commercial paper market if they need any additional short term cash.

Property, Plant and Equipment

They are carried at their original cost, less accumulated depreciation. Depreciation is what occurs as the plant and equipment wear out little by little; every year, a charge is taken against the plant and equipment.

Goodwill

When Exxon buys XYZ oil company and Exxon pays a price excess XYZ's book value, the excess is recorded on Exxon's balance sheet under the heading of Goodwill.

Intangible Assets

These are assets we can't physically touch; these include patents, copyrights, trademarks, franchises, brandnames, and the like.
Companies are not allowed to carry internally developed intangible assets on their balance sheet. However, intangible assets that are acquired from a third party are carried on the balance sheet at their fair value.
If an asset has an finite life 'as a pattent does' it is amortized over the course of its useful life charge made to the income statement and the balance sheet.
Coca-Cola's brand name is worth in excess of $100 billion, yet because it is an internally developed brand name, it's real value as an intangible asset is not reflected on the balance sheet.

Long Term Investments

In this asset account the value of long-term assests are recorded (longer than a year), such as stocks, bonds, and real estate. This account includes investments in the company's affiliates and subsidiaries.
These assets are carries on the books at their cost or market price, wichever is lower. But it cannot be marketed to a price above cost even if the investments have appreciated in value.

Other Long Term Assets

Think of this account as a giant pool of long term assets, that didn't make it into the other catogories. For instance: pre-paidexpenses and tax recoveries that are due to be received in the coming years.

Total Assets

Add current assets to long-term assets and we get the company's total assets.

Liabilities

Liabilities are debt and obligations that the company owes.

Accounts Payable

Accounts payable is money owed to suppliers that have provided goods and services to the company on credit. We order 1,000 pounds of coffee, and they send it to us along with a bill/invoice. The bill/invoice for the 1,000 pounds of coffee is an account payable.

Accrued Expenses

Accrued Expenses are liabilities that the company has incurred, but has yet to be invoiced for.
These include: sales tax payable, wages payable, and accrued rent payable.

Short Term Debt

Short Term Debt is money that is owed by the corporation and due within the year. This includes commercial paper and short term bank loans.

Long Term Debt Due

This is long term debt that is coming due and has to be paid off in the current year.

Current Liabilities

Current Liabilities are debt and obligations that the company owes that are coming due within the fiscal year.

Long Term Debt

Long term debt means debt that matures any time out past a year. On the balance sheet it comes under the heading of long term liablities.

Total Shareholders Equity

Shareholders' equity also known as Net Worth or Book Value.
Shareholders' equity is equal to the company's total assets minus its total liabilities.
That happens to equal the total sums of preferred and common stock, plus paid in capital, plus retained earnings, less treasury stock.
Shareholders' equity has three sources. One is the capital that was originally raised selling preferred and common stock to the public.
The second is any later sales of preferred and common stock to the public after the company is up and running.
The third, and most important to us, is the accumulation of retained earnings.

Preferred Stock

Preferred shareholders don't have voting rights.

Common Stock

Common stock represents ownership in the company. Common stock owners are the owners of the company and have the right to elect a board of directors, which in turn will hire a CEO to run a company.

Additional Paid in Capital

From a balance sheet perspective preferred and common stocks are carried on the books at their par value, and any money in excess of par that was paid in when the company sold the stock will be carried on the books as "paid in capital."

Retained Earnings

A company's net earnings can be paid out as dividends or used to buy back the company's shares, or it can be retained to keep the business growing.
When they are retained in the business, they are added to an account on the balance sheet, under shareholders' equity called retained earnings.
Retained earnings is an accumulated number, which means that each year's new retained earnings are added to the total of accumulated earnings from all prior years.
If a company is not making additions to its retained earnings, it is not growing it's net earnings.

Treasury Stock

When a company buys back its own shares, it can do two things with them. It can cancel them or it can retain them with the possibility of reissuing them later on. If it cancels them the shares cease to exist. But if it keeps them, with the possibility of reissuing them later on, they are carried on the balance sheet under shareholders equity as treasury stocks.
Treasury Stock are carried on the balance sheet at a negative value because they represent a reduction in the shareholders'equity.
